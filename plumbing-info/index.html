The State of Sanitation in the Roman Empire
3100 BC

Public Toilet Problems

Roman public toilets were often situated in dark and dingy places with small toilets that could accommodate up to 20 people at a time. “Toilet paper” was often just a communally shared stick or sponge that would be used for wiping. Also, flames could erupt from the seat openings due to a build-up of hydrogen sulfide and methane from an excess of human excrement.

Private Toilet Problems

Houses and facilities with private toilets were not without their flaws either. Most notable being that the toilets would be located within the kitchen area of a domicile. Additionally, while some public toilets were connected to the main sewer lines, often private toilets lacked this feature given Roman’s fear of rats and other animals making their way into their home. Interestingly, the collected waste was used in gardens or sold to farmers for growing their crops and food.

The State of Sanitation in Greece
2200 - 1450 BC

While early Ancient Greeks lacked modern plumbing, often using chamber pots which was then used as fertilizer for their fields. As Greek civilization advanced and cities grew their plumbing and sanitation system became complex as is the case what is called the Great Drain located in Athens which started construction in circa the 5th century BC. The Great Drain itself was connected to smaller circular terracotta pipes and channels.

The State of Sanitation Near East Cultures
3100 BC

One of the first ancient cultures to create plumbing and sanitation systems, as clay sewer pits have been found in the ancient trade city of Eshnunna and the Temple of Bel at Nippur around 4600BC. However, these examples were engineering and architectural marvels, and the every-day Mesopotamian usually possessed a room in their house that acted as a bathroom and would be periodically cleaned and then dumped at a communal cesspit.

Indus Valley Civilization
2600 BC

While little is known of the Indus River Valley civilization, their culture had highly advanced plumbing and sanitation systems. Each house had drains which connected to bigger sewage outlets in the city proper. Presenting a sophistication that rivaled Roman sanitation systems almost 1,800 years later. Furthermore, much like Roman culture, the Indus River Valley culture seems elevated the social aspects of cleanliness with “The Great Bath” built in one of their largest cities, Mohenjo-Daro. Even go so far as to have a primitive form of waterproofing the outer and inner walls with asphaltum.

The State of Sanitation in Egypt
3100 BC

Given the primacy of religious purification in everyday life, Egyptian’s sanitation and plumbing practices served parallel functions in a religious context as Egyptians conception of death was simply passing into a new life; unsurprisingly, tombs have been found sophisticated bathrooms like the mortuary temple at Abusir. The temple had recessed niches that lead to basins which were closed with a lead stopper attached to chain. This basin would then be drained with an equally sophisticated copper pipe to a trough below.

MIDDLE AGES SANITATION

As the Roman empire gave way to a fractious Europe, the states that eventually grew out didn’t possess the same attitudes towards bathing. They also didn’t have the political capital to invest in building large scale plumbing and sanitation systems.

Much like ancient civilizations before, European states reverted to using chamber pots and cesspits as the primary means of waste disposal.

However, as the cities of Europe and outbreaks of cholera and typhoid grew, so did the need to develop a more robust management system.

The ruling class often had their castles outfitted with a garderobe, which was a room fitted with a seat. It discharged any waste outside into a cesspit or moat, depending on the architecture of the castle.

In 1272, Dubrovnik, a city in Croatia, decreed the construction of septic tanks and dedicated plumbing and channels for the removing of standing and waste water.

THE BLACK DEATH

The patchwork system of various methods of waste management came to a head with the Black Death. The improper sanitation often exacerbating the spread of disease throughout Eurasia until the overestimated 60% percent of the European population had died.

Shortly after the effects of the Black Death had subsided, Paris began construction of the first “closed sewer” system in Europe around 1360 that ran for 300m through what it now Rue Montramonte.

Did you know?

The English also took notice of the amount of human waste being dumped into rivers and streams, most notable the River Thames and in 1388 the English Parliament declared;

“Item, that so much dung and filth of the garbage and entrails be cast and put into ditches, rivers, and other waters… so that the air there is grown greatly corrupt and infected, and many maladies and other intolerable diseases do daily happen… it is accorded and assented, that the proclamation be made as well in the city of London, as in other cities, boroughs, and towns through the realm of England, where it shall be needful that all they who do cast and lay all such annoyances, dung, garbages, entrails, and other ordure, in ditches, rivers, waters, and other places aforesaid, shall cause them utterly to be removed, avoided, and carried away, every one upon pain to lose and forfeit to our Lord the King the sum of 20pounds…”

  • HYGIENE AS A CONCEPT

    18th Century

    Much like the 17th century, bathing was still not an everyday occurrence but more so than it was 100 years prior.

    Medical literature of the time also differed on issues of hygiene, with some still believing that bathing would invite disease while others believed that disease and sickness could be traced back to dirty clothes and bodies.

    Those doctors advocated for frequent and often sponging and changing not only one’s clothes but bed linens as well.

    HYGIENE AS A CONCEPT

  • Germ Theory of Disease

    Most notable of these doctors was Austrian physician Marcus von Plenciz whose 1762 book Opera medico-physica outlined what can be described as a germ theory of disease; though there had been earlier physicians advocating similar theories of contagain since 1052.

  • A representation by Robert Seymour of the cholera epidemic depicts the spread of the disease in the form of poisonous air.

  • Interestingly, wealthy families in America experimented with a could be considered a primitive shower. This shower would be outside with a basin full of water. The user would pull a cord or rope which would then disperse through a hose and into a colander.

From Elizabeth Drinker’s diary, the children seemed to enjoy using it, whereas, Elizabeth took a year to try out the device; commenting that “I bore it better than expected…not having been wet all over at once, for twenty eight years past”
